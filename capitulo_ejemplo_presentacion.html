Kinematics: The Study of Motion

🎯 Objetivos de aprendizaje

By the end of this chapter the student will be able to:

  • Distinguish between position, displacement, and distance traveled.
  • Calculate average and instantaneous velocity.
  • Solve URM and UARM problems.
  • Apply the free-fall equations.
  • Interpret position-time and velocity-time graphs.

1. Position and displacement

Kinematics is the branch of mechanics that describes the motion of bodies without considering the forces that produce it. To describe motion we need to define a reference frame.

📖 Definición
Position
Coordinate that indicates the location of an object relative to a reference point (origin) at a given instant. It is a vector quantity represented as x(t) in one dimension.
📖 Definición
Displacement
Change in the position of an object: Δx=xf​−xi​. It is a vector from the initial point to the final point, independent of the path taken.
⚠️ Advertencia

Do not confuse displacement with distance traveled. Displacement is a vector (has direction and can be negative); distance is a scalar (always positive). A runner who completes one lap of a 400 m track has distance traveled = 400 m but displacement = 0.

2. Velocity

📖 Definición
Average velocity
Ratio of displacement to time interval: vˉ=ΔtΔx​. It is a vector quantity. SI units are m/s.
📖 Definición
Instantaneous velocity
Limit of average velocity as the time interval approaches zero: v=lim(Δt→0)ΔtΔx​=dtdx​. It corresponds to the slope of the position-time curve at each instant.
💡 Concepto clave

In a position vs. time graph, the slope of the curve at any point is the instantaneous velocity. A straight line indicates constant velocity; a curve indicates acceleration.

✏️ Ejemplo Average velocity calculation

A cyclist starts at position x1​=2km at t1​=0min and arrives at x2​=14km at t2​=30min.

📐 Ecuación
vˉ=ΔtΔx​=30−014−2​minkm​=12/30=0.4minkm​=24hkm​
📜 Contexto histórico Galileo and the measurement of motion
👤 Galileo Galilei📅 1604

In 17th-century Italy there were no precise chronometers. Galileo needed to measure time accurately to study falling bodies, but the clocks of his era were too imprecise.

His solution was ingenious: instead of dropping objects vertically (too fast to measure), he built polished inclined planes to "dilute" gravity. He used a water container with a small hole — the water that flowed out during the fall was weighed, and the weight was proportional to the elapsed time.

With this rudimentary method he demonstrated that the distance covered in free fall is proportional to the square of the time (d∝t2), one of the first quantitative laws of physics and the foundation of modern kinematics.

3. Acceleration

📖 Definición
Acceleration
Rate of change of velocity with respect to time: a=ΔtΔv​. Measured in m/s² in SI. It can be positive (speeding up), negative (slowing down), or zero (constant velocity).
📝 Nota

In everyday language "accelerate" means to go faster. In physics, a negative acceleration does not necessarily mean braking — it depends on the direction of motion. An object moving to the left and accelerating to the left has negative velocity and negative acceleration, but its speed increases.

4. Uniform Rectilinear Motion (URM)

💡 Concepto clave

In URM, velocity is constant (a=0). Position varies linearly with time:

📐 Ecuación
x=x0​+v⋅t
⚖️ Comparación URM vs. UARM
Property URM UARM
Velocity Constant Changes uniformly
Acceleration 0 Constant (≠ 0)
x-t graph Straight line Parabola
v-t graph Horizontal line Inclined line
Position equation x=x0​+v⋅t x=x0​+v0​⋅t+21​⋅a⋅t2

5. Uniformly Accelerated Rectilinear Motion (UARM)

The UARM equations are:

📐 Ecuación
v=v0​+a⋅t
x=x0​+v0​⋅t+21​⋅a⋅t2
v2=v02​+2⋅a⋅(x−x0​)
🧠 Mnemotécnico

The 3 UARM equations — each one omits a variable:

  • Equation 1 omits position (x does not appear)
  • Equation 2 omits final velocity (v does not appear)
  • Equation 3 omits time (t does not appear)

Trick: identify what data you are missing in the problem and use the equation that does not need it.

🧩 Ejercicio Braking a car

A car travels at 90 km/h when the driver brakes uniformly and stops in 5 seconds. Calculate: a) The braking acceleration. b) The distance covered during braking.

✅ Solución

Data: v0​=90hkm​=25sm​, v=0sm​, t=5s

a) a=(v−v0​)/t=(0−25)/5=−5sm​2

b) x=v0​⋅t+21​⋅a⋅t2=25⋅5+21​⋅(−5)⋅52=125−62.5=62.5m

🔬 Aplicación Braking distance in road safety

Braking distance is critical in traffic engineering. At 90 km/h, the car in the example needs 62.5 m to stop — not counting the driver's reaction time (≈ 1 s), which adds another 25 m. The total stopping distance would be ~87.5 m. This calculation determines the minimum following distance between vehicles and the length of braking zones on highways.

6. Free fall

💡 Concepto clave

Free fall is a special case of UARM where the acceleration is gravity:

📐 Ecuación
a=g≈9.8sm​2
💬 Dato adicional Did you know?

In 1971, astronaut David Scott simultaneously dropped a hammer and a feather on the Moon (no atmosphere). Both reached the ground at exactly the same time, confirming Galileo's prediction 367 years later — but this time on another world.

🧩 Ejercicio Object falling from a building

A ball is dropped from the roof of a 45 m tall building (v0​=0). Calculate: a) The time it takes to reach the ground. b) The impact velocity. (Use g=10sm​2 for simplicity)

✅ Solución

Data: y0​=45m, v0​=0, a=g=10sm​2

a) y=21​⋅g⋅t2 → 45=21​⋅10⋅t2 → t2=9 → t = 3 s

b) v=g⋅t=10⋅3=30sm​ (108 km/h)

7. Interpreting graphs

💡 Concepto clave

Graphs are fundamental tools in kinematics:

  • In an x vs. t graph: the slope is velocity; curvature indicates acceleration.
  • In a v vs. t graph: the slope is acceleration; the area under the curve is displacement.

  2. Draw the corresponding x-t graph
  3. Calculate the total displacement (area under the curve)

📋 Resumen del capítulo
  • Position is the location of an object relative to the origin; displacement is the change in position (Δx, vector); distance is the total path length (scalar).
  • Average velocity is vˉ=ΔtΔx​; instantaneous velocity is the derivative dtdx​ (slope in x-t graph).
  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity (a=ΔtΔv​).
  • In URM: v=const, x=x0​+v⋅t.
  • In UARM: a=const, with three fundamental equations relating x, v, a, and t.
  • Free fall is UARM with a=g≈9.8sm​2. All bodies fall equally without air resistance.
  • x-t and v-t graphs contain all kinematic information: slopes and areas reveal velocity, acceleration, and displacement.
❓ Pregunta
Desarrollo
  1. A train starts from rest and accelerates at 2sm​2 for 20 s. It then maintains constant speed for 60 s. Finally, it brakes uniformly and stops in 10 s. Calculate the total distance traveled.
  2. Explain why braking distance quadruples when speed doubles.
Ver respuesta modelo
  1. Phase 1: x=21​⋅2⋅202=400m (vf​=40sm​). Phase 2: x=40⋅60=2400m. Phase 3: x=40⋅10+21​⋅(−4)⋅102=200m. Total = 3000 m.
  1. Because braking distance depends on v2 (from the equation v2=v02​+2⋅a⋅d). If v doubles, v2 quadruples, and with the same deceleration the distance quadruples.
❓ Pregunta
Selección múltiple

An object is thrown vertically upward. At the highest point of its trajectory:

Gravitational acceleration never vanishes
Correct — velocity is momentarily zero but gravity keeps acting
Velocity is maximum at launch, not at the highest point
Both cannot be maximum simultaneously in this case
❓ Pregunta
Selección múltiple

In a velocity vs. time graph, displacement is obtained by calculating:

The slope gives acceleration, not displacement
That gives the initial velocity
That is just a single value
Correct — the integral of v(t) is displacement
❓ Pregunta
Verdadero o falso

In the absence of air resistance, a heavier object falls faster than a lighter one.

Incorrect — all objects fall with the same acceleration regardless of mass. Galileo demonstrated this in the 17th century.
❓ Pregunta
Caso aplicado

A traffic forensics expert arrives at an accident scene. The skid marks on the asphalt measure 40 m. The friction coefficient between tires and dry asphalt is μ=0.7 (deceleration ≈6.86sm​2). The speed limit in that area is 60 km/h.

a) At what speed was the vehicle traveling before braking? b) Was it exceeding the speed limit? c) If the pavement were wet (μ=0.4, deceleration ≈3.92sm​2), how long would the skid mark be at the same initial speed?

Ver respuesta modelo

a) v=2×6.86×40​=23.4sm​=84.3hkm​

b) Yes, exceeding the limit by 24.3 km/h (84.3 vs 60 km/h)

c) d=v2/(2⋅a)=23.42/(2×3.92)=69.9m — nearly double than on dry road
